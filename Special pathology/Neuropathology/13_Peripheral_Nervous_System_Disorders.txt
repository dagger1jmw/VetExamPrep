PERIPHERAL NERVE SYSTEM (PNS)

==============================================================
DIVISIONS OF THE PERIPHERAL NERVOUS SYSTEM
==============================================================
  1. SENSORIMOTOR DIVISION — sensory and motor nerves
  2. AUTONOMOUS DIVISION — sympathetic and parasympathetic system
  3. ENTERIC DIVISION — myenteric (Auerbach) plexus and submucosal (Meissner) plexus

==============================================================
DYSAUTONOMIAS
==============================================================
Definition: degeneration of neurons in the ganglia of the enteric division of the PNS
  - Can be acquired or hereditary

--- EQUINE DYSAUTONOMIA (EQUINE GRASS SICKNESS) — ACQUIRED ---
Cause: ingestion of food contaminated with Clostridium botulinum type C

Macro: NO macroscopic changes on PNS

Micro:
  - Chromatolysis, degeneration: neurons of autonomic and enteric ganglia within the ileum
  - Chromatolysis, pyknosis, vacuolar change in affected neurons

Clinical signs:
  - Dysphagia
  - Paralytic ileus
  - Gut stasis (colic)
  - VERY BAD PROGNOSIS, very high mortality

==============================================================
MYASTHENIA GRAVIS
==============================================================

--- INHERITED FORM ---
  - Genetic disease
  - Too small number of acetylcholine receptors on the muscular plates (endplates) of neuromuscular junctions
  - No macroscopic or microscopic changes in CNS or PNS

--- ACQUIRED FORM ---
  - Associated with: thymoma, thymic hyperplasia
  - Development of ANTIBODIES that bind to acetylcholine receptors on postsynaptic muscle membranes
  - Type: TYPE II HYPERSENSITIVITY
  - No macro or micro changes in CNS or PNS

Clinical signs:
  - Skeletal muscle weakness (fatigable)
  - Megaoesophagus development

==============================================================
BOTULISM
==============================================================
Agent: intoxication with exotoxin of Clostridium botulinum types A, B, C, D
Species most commonly affected: horses and cattle

THREE TYPES REGARDING PORTAL OF ENTRY:

  1. TOXIC INFECTIVE (foals):
     - Bacterial spores enter the organism
     - Proliferation and toxin production under anaerobic conditions (intestinal colonisation)

  2. ORAL INTOXICATION (adult horses):
     - Pre-formed toxins introduced through contaminated feed (silage, haylage, decomposed carcasses)

  3. TISSUE INJURY (rare):
     - Development of anaerobic conditions in deep traumatic wounds with necrosis
     - Proliferation of spores and formation of toxins in situ

MECHANISM OF ACTION OF TOXIN:
  - Exotoxin in the bloodstream enters neuromuscular junctions
  - Binds to PRESYNAPTIC CHOLINERGIC MEMBRANE RECEPTORS
  - Enters the cytosol of the nerve pedunculus
  - PERMANENTLY DISABLES the release of acetylcholine
  - Interferes with Ca²⁺-conditioned exocytosis

Result:
  - Flaccid paralysis
  - Death due to flaccid paralysis and suffocation (diaphragm failure)

Pathology:
  - NO macroscopic changes
  - NO microscopic changes

==============================================================
PERIPHERAL NERVE SHEATH TUMOURS
==============================================================
Also called: SCHWANNOMA / NEUROFIBROMA / NEURILEMOMA

Species and nerve predilections:
  DOGS: Vth cranial nerve (trigeminal), posterior cervical, anterior thoracic brachial plexus root, and skin
  CATTLE: VIIIth cranial nerve (vestibulocochlear), brachial plexus, intercostal nerves, autonomic nerves
  CATS: similar distribution to dogs

Macro:
  - Nodular thickening of the nervous trunk or nerve root
  - Can be located within the dura mater
  - Can spread through the vertebral foramen to the external environment
  - Texture: soft to hard elastic
  - Colour: white to grey

Micro — TWO PATTERNS:
  ANTONI A TYPE:
    - Densely cellular
    - Monomorphous spindle cells
    - Variety of rich collagen stroma

  ANTONI B TYPE:
    - Less densely cellular
    - Cell aggregates within abundant MYXOMATOUS STROMA

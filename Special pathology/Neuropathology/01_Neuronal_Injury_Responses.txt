MICROSCOPIC CHANGES VISIBLE IN THE BODY OF NEURONAL CELLS
  a. Central chromatolysis — due to axonal injury
  b. Ischemic change of cells (necrosis)
  c. Increase of neuronal cell body in lysosomal storage diseases
  d. Lipofuscin pigment accumulation during aging
  e. Neurofilament accumulation in certain degenerative neuronal diseases
  f. Formation of inclusions in certain viral diseases

==============================================================
NEURONAL CELLULAR RESPONSES TO INJURY
==============================================================

CENTRAL CHROMATOLYSIS
  - Histopathological changes in the body of neurons when chromatin and cell nucleus are displaced to the periphery due to swelling of the cell
  - Pathogenesis: includes (besides traumatic injury) vitamin B3 deficiency

TOTAL CHROMATOLYSIS
  - Disruption and loss of Nissl's substance due to axotomy (breakdown), ischemia, toxic insult, viral infection
  - Rarely reversible; more often the first stage of apoptosis of neurons

VACUOLISATION
  - Neuronal vacuolisation characteristic of TSE (transmissible spongiform encephalopathies / prion diseases)

VIRAL INCLUSION
  - Intracytoplasmic (e.g. Negri bodies in rabies) or intranuclear

NECROSIS
  - Process that affects cell groups
  - Hydropic degeneration, nuclear fragmentation, cell lysis due to damage of the cell membrane
  - Cellular debris causes inflammatory response

APOPTOSIS
  - Process that affects individual isolated cells
  - Cell death does NOT trigger an inflammatory response

ISCHEMIC NEURON (Neuronal Necrosis)
  - Cellular morphology details are lost in the neuron body
  - Neuron = reduced size, triangular, eosinophilic with condensed nucleus
  - Ischemic homogenization of the cell = necrosis

NEURONOPHAGIA
  - The neuron body is surrounded by macrophages/microglia that remove cellular detritus

EXCITOTOXICITY
  - Mechanism often involved in the development of neuronal necrosis
  - Based on the action of neuroexcitatory neurotransmitters (amino acids): GLUTAMATE and ASPARTATE
  - Sequence:
      Initial injury → persistent activation of glutamate receptors on affected neurons
      → influx of extracellular Ca2+ ions
      → impaired mitochondrial functioning
      → development of ROS (reactive oxygen species)
      → damage to lipid membranes of nerve cells

WALLERIAN DEGENERATION
  - Changes occurring in a nerve after axon transection or breakdown in both PNS and CNS

  Changes PROXIMAL to the site of injury:
    - Central chromatolysis (coagulation of soma, dispersion of Nissl's substance, peripheral displacement of the nucleus)

  Changes DISTAL from injury site:
    - Wallerian degeneration: axonal swelling and fragmentation of myelin → degeneration and phagocytosis by microglia and macrophages

  CNS vs PNS difference:
    - CNS: process is slower, very limited regeneration
    - PNS: if there is not too much gap between the cut ends AND endoneural tubes are preserved → good prospects for regeneration

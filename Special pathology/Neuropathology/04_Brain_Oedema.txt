FOUR TYPES OF BRAIN OEDEMA:

==============================================================
1. CYTOTOXIC OEDEMA
==============================================================
Definition: Cell swelling with NORMAL blood vessel permeability

Mechanism:
  - Hydropic degeneration of CNS cells → intracellular fluid accumulation
  - Energy deficit (NRJ deficit)
  → disruption of Na+/K+ pump
  → elevated water and sodium inside cells
  → reduced volume of extracellular fluid
  → increased ion concentration
  → transfer of capillary fluid and CSF into extracellular space of cells

Key features:
  - Hematoencephalic barrier REMAINS INTACT
  - Affects BOTH white and grey matter
  - Gyri and sulci become indistinct and FLATTENED
  - Swelling develops within seconds after injury
  - Macro appearance: similar to vasogenic oedema

Causes: hypoxia, ischemia, hypothermia, intoxication

==============================================================
2. VASOGENIC OEDEMA
==============================================================
Definition: Tissue swelling due to increase of extracellular fluid volume

Mechanism:
  - Breakdown of hematoencephalic barrier
  → plasma and electrolytes enter the perivascular and extracellular brain space
  → preferentially affects WHITE MATTER

Causes:
  - Inflammation, haematoma, contusion, infarction
  - Cerebral hypertension, neoplasia

==============================================================
3. HYDROSTATIC INTERSTITIAL OEDEMA
==============================================================
Mechanism:
  - Hydrocephalus → increased CSF volume and pressure
  → leaking of CSF through the ependymal layer into the white matter of the brain

==============================================================
4. HYPO-OSMOTIC OEDEMA
==============================================================
Cause: Water intoxication / sodium deficiency

Mechanism:
  - Irregular ion concentration and distribution between blood and nerve tissue
  - Hyperosmolarity develops first (e.g. sodium accumulation)
  - Then rapid shift to hypoosmolarity
  - Brain unable to adapt fast enough and cannot resolve pre-accumulated ions/osmotically active molecules
  - Water suddenly enters the brain → oedema

Clinical context: sodium chloride toxicosis (pigs, poultry) — water deprivation followed by unrestricted access

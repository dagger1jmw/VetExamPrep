==============================================================
II. DEFENCE MECHANISMS OF THE CNS
==============================================================
a. Skin: structural and functional barrier
b. Calvaria, vertebrae: structural barrier
c. Meninges and CSF: structural and functional barrier
d. Barrier system:
     - Hematoencephalic barrier (blood-brain barrier)
     - Blood-CSF barrier
     - Glia limitans
e. Microglia and trafficking macrophages
f. Immunological responses

HEMATOENCEPHALIC BARRIER (Blood-Brain Barrier):
  - Selective regulation of extracellular space
  - Isolation from sudden biochemical changes in peripheral circulation
  - Active regulation of transport of proteins and hydrophilic molecules
  - Diffusion of small lipid molecules
  - Receptor transport: insulin, transferrin, glucose, purine, amino acids
  - Negative charge of the basement membrane PREVENTS entry of anionic molecules

Sub-anatomical note:
  - In several areas, pia mater = one cell layer with fenestrated capillaries
  - Arteries penetrating cerebral cortex enter pia mater and glia limitans before becoming capillaries
  - Pia mater and glia limitans separate the CNS from the subarachnoid space

EPENDYMAL BARRIER (CSF Barrier):
  - Barrier between CSF and brain
  - MORE PERMEABLE than other CNS barriers
  - Based on mechanical barrier formed by ependymal cells and astrocytic processes

==============================================================
III. PORTALS OF ENTRY INTO THE CNS
==============================================================

a. DIRECT EXTENSION
   - Penetrating wounds
   - Middle/inner ear infection
   - Sinuses/nasal cavity
   - Bone/bone marrow (osteomyelitis)
   - Skull trauma
   - Neoplasia

b. HEMATOGENOUS
   - Localisation within the meningeal capillary network
   - Localisation in the CNS parenchyma
   - Localisation in the choroid plexus capillary network
   - Extension from plexus into CSF

c. LEUKOCYTE TRAFFICKING
   - Extension within macrophages and lymphocytes
   - Examples: FeLV, Blastomyces dermatitidis — use monitoring cells for CNS entry (Trojan horse)

d. RETROGRADE AXONAL TRANSPORT
   - Examples: Rabies virus, Listeria monocytogenes
   - Primarily infect tissues rich in terminal plates of sensory and motor neurons
   - Travel retrogradely along the axon to the CNS

==============================================================
RESULT OF INFLAMMATORY RESPONSE IN THE CNS
==============================================================
Goal: dilution and removal of the infectious agent

Types of CNS inflammatory response:
  - SEROUS / SUPPURATIVE (most bacteria)
  - EOSINOPHILIC (NaCl toxicity, larval migration)
  - LYMPHOCYTIC AND MONOCYTIC (viruses, protozoa)
  - GRANULOMATOUS (fungi, protozoa, some bacteria)

If pathogens breach the barrier system:
  - Surveillance patrol of cytokines, chemokines, receptors activates acute response
  - Passage of neutrophils across hematoencephalic barrier
  - → INFLAMMATION, OEDEMA, HAEMORRHAGE

IV. SPECIFIC DISEASES AND DISORDERS OF THE CNS — MALFORMATIONS

==============================================================
NEURAL TUBE CLOSURE DEFECTS (DYSRAPHIA)
==============================================================
Cause:
  - Abnormal interaction of the neuroepithelium (neurons, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, ependymal cells) with notochordal and mesenchymal cells in early development
  - 4 locations at closure initiation sites = locus-specific dysraphic anomalies
      Closing point 1: posterior neuropore
      Closing sites II, III, and IV: anterior neuropores
  - Defect of genes regulating the metabolic pathway of folic acid and folate transport
  - Inherited aetiology or intrauterine viral infections

ANENCEPHALIC / PORENCEPHALIC HYPOPLASIA
  - Error at closure sites II and IV
  - Abnormal development of the rostral side of the neural tube
  - Lack of cerebral hemisphere; brain stem preserved in varying degrees

MENINGOENCEPHALOCELE AND CRANIUM BIFIDUM
  - Medial defect of the cranial wall with protrusion of meninges and brain tissue
  - Defect covered by skin forming a sac (-cele) = meningoencephalocele
  - Defect covered by skin and lined from inside by meninges = meningocele

MENINGOMYELOCELE AND SPINA BIFIDA
  - Vertebral counterpart of cranium bifidum

HYDROMYELIA
  - Abnormal extension of the central spinal canal
  - Forms a huge elongated cyst filled with CSF
  - Bordered by ependymal cells (distinguishes from syringomyelia)

SYRINGOMYELIA
  - Formation of a cyst or tubular cavitation WITHIN the spinal cord
  - NOT bordered by ependymal cells
  - NOT related to the central canal

==============================================================
NEURONAL MIGRATION DISORDERS
==============================================================

LISSENCEPHALY / AGYRIA / PACHYGYRIA
  - Smooth brain surface without gyri and sulci
  - Affects part or entire cerebral cortex
  - Cortex is thicker; histologically missing laminar neuronal arrangement
  - Genetically conditioned defect of the signal pathway leading neurons to proper locations during brain development

==============================================================
ENCEPHALOCLASTIC DEFECTS
==============================================================

PORENCEPHALY
  - Formation of small liquid-filled cavities in the brain
  - Results in cleavage or cyst in the wall of the hemisphere in contact with the subarachnoid space or lateral ventricle
  - Caused by destruction of immature neuroblasts

HYDRANENCEPHALY
  - Formation of large cavities in the brain filled with liquid in areas of WHITE MATTER
  - Heavier form of the encephaloclastic disease

==============================================================
CEREBELLAR HYPOPLASIA
==============================================================
Definition: small, undeveloped cerebellum; primarily due to intrauterine viral infections

Causative agents — PARVOVIRUSES:
  - Feline panleukopenia virus (kittens)
  - Canine parvovirus (puppies)

Causative agents — PESTIVIRUSES:
  - Bovine viral diarrhoea virus (BVD) — calves
  - Classical swine fever virus — piglets

Mechanism:
  - Infection in late gestation or early neonatal period
  - Infiltrate and destroy undifferentiated cells of the outer granular layer of the cerebellum
  - These cells should migrate inward and build the inner granular layer

Severity depends on:
  - Intensity of necrosis and vasculitis
  - Foetal/neonatal age at time of infection

Histology:
  - Loss of outer granular layer
  - Loss of Purkinje cells; malposition of Purkinje cells retained in granular layer
  - Oedema and bleeding in the white matter
  - Focal cavitation and white matter necrosis
  - Lymphocytic leptomeningitis

==============================================================
HYDROCEPHALUS
==============================================================
Definition: Abnormal accumulation of CSF in ventricular spaces and subarachnoid space

Development sequence:
  Non-absorption → increased CSF pressure in ventricles
  → widening of ventricles
  → multifocal ependymal disruption
  → CSF compresses periventricular white matter
  → hydrostatic oedema of white matter
  → degeneration and atrophy of myelin and axons
  → loss of tissue and expansion of ventricles
  → compressive atrophy → NECROSIS

Most vulnerable obstruction site:
  Blockage order: interventricular foramen → bilateral interventricular foramina → mesencephalic aqueduct → lateral opening of IV ventricle

--- 1. CONGENITAL HYDROCEPHALUS ---
Clinical signs:
  - Most common in brachycephalic or miniature breeds
  - Manifestation in first 3 months of life
  - Poor motor skills, inability to learn, sensory disturbances, dullness, aggression attacks, circling

Morphological characteristics:
  - Expansion of ventricular system proximal to obstruction
  - Thinning of white matter; grey matter thickness preserved
  - Atrophy of interventricular septum and hippocampus
  - Flattening of cortical gyri
  - Herniation of parahippocampal gyri under tentorium
  - Herniation of cerebellum through foramen magnum
  - Skull bones thin and often not sutured

Types:
  COMMUNICATING: limiting arterial pulsating hydrocephalus; rare in animals; preserved communication between ventricular and subarachnoid space
    - Causes: arteritis, subarachnoid bleeding, meningitis

  NON-COMMUNICATING: consequence of obstruction within the ventricular system
    - Mesencephalic aqueduct most vulnerable to obstruction
    - Disruption of CSF resorption

  EX-VACUO / COMPENSATING: occurs secondary to loss or lack of brain tissue; part of progression of hydranencephaly

--- 2. ACQUIRED HYDROCEPHALUS ---
UNCOMMUNICATING: due to injuries of ependyma or obstruction of IV ventricle opening, cerebral aqueduct, or interventricular foramen
  - No change in skull bones
  - Causes: compression by abscesses and neoplasms, blockage due to inflammation, cholesteatomas of choroid plexus in horses

EX-VACUO / COMPENSATING: secondary to loss of neural tissue
  - Spreading ventricles into white substance
  - Unilateral or bilateral hydrocephalus — loss of white and grey matter
  - Caused by: brain infarction (irregular shape) or brain aging (atrophy, ceroid lipofuscinosis)

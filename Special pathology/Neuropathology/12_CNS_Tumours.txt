VII. TUMOURS

==============================================================
EMBRYONAL / PRIMITIVE NEOPLASIA
==============================================================

--- PRIMITIVE NEUROECTODERMAL TUMOUR (PNET) ---
  - Exceptionally rare (calves, primates)
  - Young animals; aggressive biological behaviour
  - Origin: primitive embryonic stem cells

--- MALIGNANT RHABDOID TUMOUR ---
  - Described in kidneys and brain (people); subcutis, muscle, and brain (dog and cat)
  - Extremely rare; young animals; aggressive biological behaviour
  - Origin: embryonic stem cells — large circular to polyhedral cells

--- MEDULLOBLASTOMA ---
  Species: dogs, cats, cows, pigs
  Age: rare in young animals; rarely in old animals
  Behaviour: metastasis via CSF

  Location: CEREBELLUM

  Macro:
    - Well-circumscribed, soft
    - Without bleeding and cystic structures
    - Can cause hydrocephalus
    - Locally invasive

  Micro:
    - Highly cellular neoplasia
    - Cells with round to elongated nuclei and small amount of cytoplasm

  Origin: primitive neuroepithelial cells (neurons) of the ROOF OF THE IV BRAIN VENTRICLE

==============================================================
GLIAL TUMOURS (GLIOMAS)
==============================================================

--- ASTROCYTOMA ---
  Classification: according to degree of differentiation
    - Diffuse astrocytoma
    - Anaplastic astrocytoma
    - Glioblastoma multiforme
    - ALL FORMS ARE MALIGNANT

  Note: degree of malignancy is INVERSELY PROPORTIONAL to degree of differentiation

  Species: most common in DOGS (especially brachycephalic breeds); rarely cats

  Location: cerebral hemisphere (temporal and piriform lobes), thalamus-hypothalamus, midbrain; rarely cerebellum and spinal cord

  Macro:
    - Compressive atrophy of surrounding tissue
    - Well-differentiated forms: morphologically similar to normal CNS tissue
    - Malignant forms: infiltrative growth, necrosis, bleeding, cavitation

  Micro:
    - Large differences in appearance depending on subtype
    - SPECIFICITY: tumour cells are arranged AROUND BLOOD VESSELS (perivascular arrangement)

--- OLIGODENDROGLIOMA ---
  Species: dogs, cats, and cattle
  Note: according to some authors, the MOST COMMON neuroectodermal neoplasia of the CNS

  Location: white matter of cerebrum, brain stem, interventricular septum
  Behaviour: locally invasive; rare CSF dissemination

  Macro: usually well-restricted, grey or pink, soft, creamy with bleeding areas

  Micro:
    - Highly cellular
    - Expressed (hyperchromatic) nucleus
    - PALE OR TRANSLUCENT CYTOPLASM ("fried egg" appearance)

==============================================================
CHOROID PLEXUS TUMOURS
==============================================================

Species: most common in dogs, horses, cattle
Most common location: IV VENTRICLE (also possible in III and lateral ventricles)

--- CHOROID PLEXUS PAPILLOMA ---
  - Usually well-circumscribed, white to red expansive growth
  - Histo: very similar to structure and cells of normal choroid plexus

--- CHOROID PLEXUS CARCINOMA ---
  - Invasive (less circumscribed)
  - Histologically: increased mitoses, solid form of growth
  - METASTASES: inside the ventricular system or subarachnoid space

==============================================================
EPENDYMOMA
==============================================================
  Species: rare; dogs, cats, cattle, and horses
  Location: most common in LATERAL VENTRICLES; rarely III and IV ventricles and central canal of spinal cord
  Behaviour: metastasis via CSF and subarachnoid space; often causes hydrocephalus

  Macro: large intraventricular mass; usually clear edges; soft, grey-white to red; frequent necrosis and bleeding

  Micro:
    - Well vascularised, cellular
    - Form ROSETTES
    - Malignancy determined by: invasive growth, frequency of mitoses, and anaplasia

==============================================================
MESODERMAL TUMOURS
==============================================================

--- MENINGIOMA ---
  Most common MESODERMAL neoplasm of the CNS; particularly common in OLDER ANIMALS, especially CATS

  Location:
    CATS: tela choroidea, surface of hemisphere, surface of cerebellum, tentorium, brain base
    DOGS: brain base, hemisphere surface, cerebellum, tentorium, spinal cord, optic nerve sheaths

  Origin: ARACHNOIDAL CAP CELLS on the outer surface of the arachnoidea

  Growth: slow — COMPRESSIVE ATROPHY of surrounding tissue

  Histological forms (based on appearance):
    - Epithelial
    - Fibroblastic
    - Transitional
    - Psammomatous
    - Angioblastic / malignant

  Micro (regardless of histological form):
    - Cells form VORTEX / LAYER CONFIGURATION
    - Or form fascicles / bundles
    - Centre is often mineralised

--- HEMANGIOSARCOMA ---
  - Rarely primary tumour; more commonly metastatic
  - Location: interface of grey and white matter
  - Species: most commonly dogs and cats
  - BLOODY CONSISTENCY: differentiates it from metastatic melanoma

--- MAMMARY CARCINOMA ---
  - Can metastasise to the CNS

==============================================================
RABIES
==============================================================
Agent: Rhabdoviridae — one of the most prominent neurotropic viruses
Note: Precise mechanism of pathogenic action on neurons has not yet been established

Macro: most often no gross changes found

Micro:
  - Lymphocytic meningoencephalitis
  - NEGRI BODIES: intracytoplasmic eosinophilic inclusions (pathognomonic)
  - Spongiform lesions in grey matter neuropil (NOT found in dogs)

==============================================================
CANINE DISTEMPER (CDV)
==============================================================
Agent: Morbillivirus, family Paramyxoviridae — PANTROPIC virus

Pathogenesis:
  - Aerosol with virus → upper respiratory tract infection
  - → invasion of local mucosal macrophages → replication in regional lymph nodes
  - PRIMARY VIRAEMIA: other lymph nodes, spleen, thymus → immunosuppression
  - SECONDARY VIRAEMIA: infection of respiratory epithelium, GI tract mucosa, urinary bladder, and CNS
  - Infected macrophages in perivascular space → spread virus further to CNS
  - ALL CNS cells can be infected

Mechanism of CNS lesion formation:
  - Direct action of virus on oligodendrocytes → demyelination
  - Inflammatory mediators from lymphocytes, microglia, and macrophages

Macro: usually no gross changes; sometimes white matter softening

Micro:
  - Demyelination
  - Status spongiosus
  - Astrocyte hypertrophy
  - Variable neuronal degeneration
  - Inclusions: intracytoplasmic AND intranuclear primarily in ASTROCYTES (also ependymal cells and neurons)
  - Later phase: phagocytosis of myelin by microglia; variable axonal necrosis

OLD DOG ENCEPHALITIS:
  - Persistent CNS infection with DEFECTIVE FORM of CDV

==============================================================
FELINE INFECTIOUS PERITONITIS (FIP)
==============================================================
Major CNS lesion: PYOGRANULOMATOUS INFLAMMATION (vasculitis) → vascular necrosis → infarcts

Mechanism of lesion formation:
  - Development of immune complexes (virus-antibody-complement)
  - Perivascular accumulation (+ macrophages)
  - Infected macrophages: cytokine release → increased vessel permeability

TWO FORMS:
  WET (EFFUSIVE) FORM:
    - Serositis
    - Accumulation of modified transudate (abdominal and thoracic cavity)

  DRY (NON-EFFUSIVE) FORM:
    - Leptomeningitis, chorioependymitis, focal encephalomyelitis, ophthalmitis
    - Pyogranulomatous vasculitis of leptomeninges and periventricular white matter

==============================================================
BOVINE MALIGNANT CATARRHAL FEVER (BMCF)
==============================================================
Agent: Sporadic, highly fatal viral diseases of cattle and other ruminants
  - 2 forms: Ovine herpesvirus 2, Caprine herpesvirus 1/2; in wildebeest: Alcelaphine herpesvirus 1
  - Target tissue: vasculature, lymphoid organs, epithelial tissue (respiratory and digestive)
  - Virus transmitted within circulating T-lymphocytes

Key lesion: NECROTISING VASCULITIS (probably due to cellular immunity and cytotoxic lymphocyte activity)

CNS lesions:
  Macro: hyperaemia and cloudiness of leptomeninges
  Micro: nonsuppurative meningoencephalomyelitis and vasculitis; lymphocytic perivascular accumulation; necrotising vasculitis of leptomeninges and brain (white matter)

==============================================================
PSEUDORABIES (AUJESZKY'S DISEASE)
==============================================================
Agent: Pseudorabies virus (suid herpesvirus 1)

Species specificity:
  - PIGS (primary host): can die from infection; mature pigs often show inapparent infection; pigs remain persistently infected latent carriers
  - Other domestic mammals: severe, frequently fatal disease

Pathogenesis:
  - Virus enters upper respiratory tract (nasopharynx), tonsils, local lymph nodes
  - Enters sensory nerve endings → transport to trigeminal ganglia and olfactory bulb → brain
  - Glycoproteins on virus surface allow cell binding and entry

Extra-neural lesions in pigs: respiratory system, lymphoid system, digestive tract, reproductive tract

CNS lesions:
  Macro: possibly leptomeningeal congestion
  Micro:
    - Non-purulent meningoencephalomyelitis and trigeminal ganglioneuritis
    - INTRANUCLEAR EOSINOPHILIC INCLUSIONS
    - Degeneration and necrosis (neurons +/-)

==============================================================
VISNA
==============================================================
Agent: Distinct viral strain of ovine Maedi-Visna virus (MVV) complex = LENTIVIRUS, family Retroviridae

Pathogenesis:
  - Virus integrates into genome of promonocytes (bone marrow) and monocytes (blood)
  - Replication occurs ONLY AFTER these cells mature into macrophages → persistent infection
  - Virus enters CNS INSIDE MACROPHAGES (leukocyte monitoring / Trojan horse)
  - Can also infect oligodendrocytes and possibly endothelial cells

Macro: possibly yellowish-creamy areas on cross section

Micro:
  - Non-purulent encephalomyelitis (primarily subependymal white matter in brain and spinal cord)
  - Pleocytosis (increased number of leukocytes in CSF)
  - Oedema, necrosis, astrocytosis, choroiditis, nonsuppurative leptomeningitis
  - Later stage: more severe demyelination

Extra-CNS lesions: pneumonia (Maedi), mastitis, arthritis, mesangial glomerulitis

==============================================================
CAPRINE LEUKOENCEPHALOMYELITIS
==============================================================
Agent: Lentivirus closely related to MVV (sheep) = Caprine Arthritis Encephalitis (CAE)
Transmission: colostrum/milk or close contact
Mechanism: similar to VISNA
Macro and micro CNS lesions: similar to VISNA but faster appearance and more severe; onset at 2–4 months of age
Extra-neural lesions (adult goats): pneumonia, mastitis, arthritis

==============================================================
WEST NILE VIRAL ENCEPHALOMYELITIS
==============================================================
Agent: Flavivirus
Transmission: mosquitoes → birds (reservoir), horses, humans

Pathogenesis (equine encephalomyelitis):
  - Place of infection: neurons and microglia

Macro:
  - Grey matter: hyperaemia, petechiae
  - Extensive bleeding in brain stem (lower part)
  - Ventral horns of the thoracolumbar part of the spinal cord

Micro:
  - Polioencephalomyelitis (mononuclear infiltrate, rarely neutrophils, bleeding)
  - No extra-neural lesions in horses

Clinical signs: GI signs, nervous symptoms

==============================================================
OTHER NON-SUPPURATIVE INFLAMMATIONS OF BRAIN AND SPINAL CORD
==============================================================
HORSES:
  - Borna disease
  - Equine encephalomyelitis (western, eastern, Venezuelan) — Alphavirus, family Togaviridae

PIGS:
  - Teschen disease, Talfan disease, benign enzootic paresis, polioencephalomyelitis (Enterovirus)
  - Haemagglutinating encephalomyelitis (Coronavirus)
  - Classical swine fever, hog cholera (Pestivirus)

SHEEP:
  - Louping ill (Flavivirus): sheep and other animals

HUMANS:
  - Japanese encephalitis (Flavivirus): horse, swine, cattle, sheep, human

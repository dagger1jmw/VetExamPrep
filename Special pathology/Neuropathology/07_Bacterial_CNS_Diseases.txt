DISEASES CAUSED BY MICROBES — BACTERIA

==============================================================
BRAIN ABSCESSES
==============================================================
Entry routes:
  - Hematogenous (bacterial emboli via bloodstream)
  - Direct extension from adjacent infected structures

Course:
  ACUTE: tissue lysis with hyperaemic borders
  CHRONIC: limitation by fibrous capsule or astrocytic proliferation

Destruction mechanism:
  - Inflammatory products (lytic neutrophil enzymes)
  - Bacterial toxins (enzymatic action)

Effect on surrounding brain:
  - Compression → increased intracranial pressure
  - Dislocation of brain parts (cerebellar vermis, parahippocampal gyri)

==============================================================
EPENDYMITIS AND CHOROID PLEXITIS
==============================================================
  - Similar pathogens to those causing brain abscesses
  - Route: hematogenous or direct spreading
  - CSF appearance: opaque yellow-white to yellow-green (purulent)
  - Can lead to obstruction of CSF circulation → hydrocephalus

==============================================================
MENINGITIS
==============================================================
Common agents: E.coli, Streptococcus spp.
Routes: hematogenous, direct extension, leukocyte trafficking
Course: acute, subacute, or chronic
Types: leptomeningitis or pachymeningitis
Exudate: suppurative, eosinophilic, non-suppurative, or granulomatous

==============================================================
NEONATAL SEPTICAEMIA
==============================================================
Common agents:
  - E.coli
  - Streptococcus spp.
  - Salmonella spp.
  - Pasteurella spp.
  - Haemophilus spp.

==============================================================
THROMBOTIC MENINGOENCEPHALITIS (TEME) — FEEDLOT CATTLE
==============================================================
Agent: Histophilus somni (Gram-negative)
  - Can colonise the upper respiratory mucosa under normal conditions

Pathogenesis:
  - Under undetected circumstances: penetrates deeper into respiratory system
  - Leads to bacteraemia
  - Bacterial emboli reach the brain (capillaries at the white-to-grey matter border)
  - Bacteria bind to endothelial cells (via receptors)
  - Contraction and desquamation of vascular endothelial cells
  - → Vasculitis → thrombosis → infarcts
  - Additional bacterial multiplication in infarcted tissue → further inflammation

Macro:
  - Irregular, small to large foci of bleeding and necrosis
  - Randomly scattered on surface and in parenchyma
  - Most common location: cerebrum at grey/white matter border
  - Brain oedema, leptomeningitis

Micro:
  - Vasculitis and perivasculitis (neutrophils, macrophages)
  - Vascular necrosis → thrombosis and infarction
  - Sometimes: Gram-negative bacterial colonies visible

==============================================================
LISTERIOSIS — Listeria monocytogenes
==============================================================
Source of infection: poor-quality silage (inadequately fermented, high pH)

Portal of entry and CNS breakthrough:
  Oral mucosa
  → invasion of sensory and motor branches of n. trigeminus (trigeminal nerve)
  → migration via sensory axons to trigeminal ganglion / brain-spinal cord
  → migration via motor axons directly to midbrain, spinal cord
  → rostral and caudal extension of bacteria in the brain
  → highest bacterial concentration = central grey and white matter of PONS AND MEDULLA
  → possible extension to: cerebellum and cranial part of spinal cord

Cells infected: neurons, microglia, epithelial cells of choroid plexus, macrophages

Pathogenesis (2 key enzymes):
  - PHOSPHOLIPASE: degradation of sphingomyelin and phospholipids — essential for interstitial spread of bacteria
  - LISTERIOLYSIN (HAEMOLYSIN): allows intracellular replication

  → POWERFUL BODY IMMUNE RESPONSE
  → Mechanism of axonal damage and neuronal death: activity of inflammation products, phospholipase, and haemolysin

Macro:
  - Usually negative (no gross changes)
  - Sometimes: opacity of leptomeninges, focal necrosis, opaque CSF

Micro (progression):
  - Beginning: loose clusters of microglia cells and neutrophils → abscess formation
  - In some foci: macrophages dominant
  - +/- vasculitis (fibrinoid necrosis/change)
  - Intralesional Gram-positive bacteria
  - Necrotic foci
  - Macrophage foci / accumulation of gitter cells (myelin phagocytosis)
  - Leptomeningitis: macrophages, lymphocytes, plasma cells, rare neutrophils

THREE CLINICAL FORMS OF LISTERIOSIS:
  1. Meningoencephalitis (most common neurological form in adult ruminants)
  2. Abortions / stillbirth (reproductive form)
  3. Septicaemia in newborns (neonatal form)

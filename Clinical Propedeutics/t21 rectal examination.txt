the clinician should stand to the side of the tail base, close to the horse’s quarters
the tail is raised with the free hand and a well-lubricated rectal sleeve is introduced into the rectum
in doing this the fingers and thumb should be brought together to form a cone for slow introduction through the anal sphincter

=> any resistance on the patient’s part usually relates to the width of the knuckles passing through the sphincter
• exploratory palpation should not be undertaken with a wide spread of fingers and thumb since this can predispose rectal trauma
• if confronted with a strong peristaltic wave, the cone shape should be resumed and the examiner should be prepared to withdraw the hand

RECTAL EXAM

Big Idea
Rectal examination is especially important in horses with
colic. The lecture states it is one of the most important
parts of the clinical work-up of a horse with colic and
should be done whenever possible, but always carefully
because it has risks.
Why it matters
Rectal examination helps the clinician assess:
• abdominal organs
• intestinal distension
• displacement of bowel
• obstruction
• reproductive tract in large animals
• prostate and anal sacs in dogs

6. Normal Structures Palpable in Horses

Twitch
• A restraint device applied to the horse’s upper lip to
help calm/restrain the horse.

// Left dorsal quadrant
Palpable structures:
• spleen
• caudal pole (tail-end portion of an organ) of left kidney
• nephrosplenic ligament (ligament connecting the left kidney and spleen)

Sedation
• Use of drugs to calm the animal and reduce
movement.

// Right / midline area
You may palpate:
• root of the mesentery (Tissue fold that suspends intestines and contains
vessels, nerves, and lymphatics.)
• caecocolic artery
• base of the cecum

Stocks
• A strong frame that safely con nes the horse during
examination.
Why restraint matters
Sudden movement can cause:
• injury to the examiner
• rectal tearing in the horse

Cecum = Large fermentation pouch in horses, located mostly on the right side.
Teniae bands = Longitudinal muscle bands on the cecum/colon. In the normal
cecum, they are relaxed and may be hooked gently without pain.

3. Technique in Horses

// Pelvic brim / ventral area

Position of clinician
• stand to the side of the tail base
• stay close to the horse’s quarters
• raise the tail with the free hand
• use a well-lubricated rectal sleeve

You may palpate:
• pelvic exure of large colon
• left ventral colon
• left dorsal colon
• small colon with fecal balls
• sometimes small intestine if contracting

How to insert the hand
• The ngers and thumb should be brought together to
form a cone shape and introduced slowly through the
anal sphincter.

Pelvic exure = A bend in the large colon near the pelvic inlet. It is important
because impactions commonly occur here.
Fecal balls = Formed feces in the small colon.

Why cone shape?
• It reduces pressure and decreases risk of trauma.

8. Findings That Suggest No Serious Lesion
Serious lesions are less likely if there is:
• easy entry
• normal feces
• relaxed abdomen
• enough space for arm movement
But this does not guarantee there is no disease.

10. Small Intestinal Abnormalities
Small intestinal obstruction / ileus
Findings:
• distended loops containing gas and uid
• tight distension in strangulating lesions
• loops may fold like an accordion
• distended small intestine usually suggests a surgical
problem
De nitions
• Obstruction = blockage of intestinal contents.
• Ileus = failure of intestinal motility.
• Strangulating lesion = obstruction with compromised
blood supply.
⸻
Anterior enteritis / proximal duodenojejunitis
Anterior enteritis = in ammation of the proximal small intestine,
especially duodenum/jejunum.
Findings:
• distended duodenum over the base of the cecum
• proximal jejunal distension
• less tight than obstruction

14. Rectal Examination in Dogs

Secondary impaction = Occurs because another disease causes
dehydration/hypovolemia and dry colon contents.

When to perform it
In any adult dog presenting with:
• tenesmus : straining to defecate
• dyschezia : painful or di cult defecation
• constipation
• hematochezia : fresh blood in feces
• diarrhea
• dysuria : di cult or painful urination
• stranguria straining/painful urination, often with small amounts

Seen with:
• grass sickness
• anterior enteritis
• ileal impaction
Important exam point
• Do not mistake secondary impaction for primary impaction, because
giving large volumes of mineral oil by nasogastric tube may risk
rupturing an already distended stomach.

This occurs when the large colon becomes trapped over the
nephrosplenic ligament.

Prostate
• In male dogs, the prostate lies along the ventral pelvic oor.
• The prostate produces some uid found in semen.

Findings:
• distended large colon lies horizontally in front of pelvic canal
• caudal to tympanitic cecum
• mesocolon can be traced to the right
• mesocolon edema suggests torsion

• severe distension

Possible abnormalities

• neoplasia

17. Anal Sacs

Rectal examination is commonly used to assess:
• uterus
• ovaries
• cervix
• bladder
• pregnancy status
• intestinal/abdominal structures

Location = Anal sacs are paired structures on the left and right side of the anus.
Common problems
• anal sac impaction
• anal sacculitis
• anal sac abscessation

Pregnancy diagnosis
• The lecture images show rectal examination of a pregnant cow, including
gravid uterus around 90 days.

Predisposed dogs
• German Shepherd dogs
• small-breed dogs may also be overrepresented

Gravid uterus = A uterus containing a pregnancy.

De nitions
Anal sac impaction
• Material in the anal sac becomes thick and cannot empty normally.
Anal sacculitis
• In ammation of the anal sac.

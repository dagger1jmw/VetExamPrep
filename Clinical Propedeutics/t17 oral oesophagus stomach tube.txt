CLINICAL TIP (Ruminants)
To pass a stomach tube in cattle, a mouth opener (rigid tube) is first inserted into the mouth along the oral cavity.
It is secured behind the ears to prevent the cow from swallowing it, although this is uncommon.
A flexible tube is then passed through this mouth opener into the esophagus.
Air is blown continuously while advancing the tube, especially at the level of the pharynx, to stimulate swallowing and help
the tube enter the esophagus.
Once past the pharynx, the tube can be advanced normally into the stomach.

Halitosis / foetor ex ore
De nition = Halitosis or foetor ex ore means unpleasant smell from the mouth.
It usually indicates abnormal bacterial growth caused by:
• tissue necrosis
• tartar
• periodontitis
• retained food in mouth or esophagus

What to remember
• Digestive disease is not only diarrhea or vomiting. It can also appear as oral
problems, swallowing problems, weight loss, abdominal pain, or abnormal feces.

Scienti c words
• Periodontitis = in ammation/infection of tissues supporting the teeth.
• Gingivitis = in ammation of the gums.
• Ketosis = metabolic state where ketones are produced; may create acetone
smell.
• Uremia = accumulation of waste products in blood due to kidney failure; breath
may smell like urine.

Regurgitation = passive expulsion of food, water, or saliva from the mouth,

• stomach disease

What to remember
• Bad breath is not just “dirty mouth.” It can indicate dental disease, retained food,
kidney disease, ketosis, or gastrointestinal disease.

7. Tube Position Check — VERY IMPORTANT
⸻
Signs the tube is in the esophagus/stomach
The tube is likely correctly placed if:
• you can see it passing through the cervical esophagus
• you can palpate it in the pharynx and cervical esophagus
• the esophagus gives moderate resistance
• suction or blowing gives expected resistance
• there is usually no coughing
• in the stomach, you can hear gas and smell stomach contents
• in ruminants, blowing into the tube produces rumen churning sounds
Important terms
• Cervical esophagus = part of the esophagus in the neck.
• Negative pressure = suction; when you aspirate and feel resistance.
• Rumen churning = rumen movement/sounds caused by rumen contents and
contractions.
⸻
Signs the tube is in the trachea :
The tube is likely incorrectly placed in the trachea if:
• you can palpate it in the pharynx but not in the cervical esophagus
• there is no resistance to passage or blowing
• you can feel breathing at the tube opening
• there is no speci c stomach smell
• the animal usually coughs, but not always
Why this matters
• If you give uid through a tube that is in the trachea, uid can enter the lungs and
cause aspiration pneumonia or death.
• Trachea = windpipe, airway leading to lungs.

11. Passing a Tube in Dogs

The lecture highlights position of the head.
Why it matters
• Correct head position helps guide the tube into the esophagus and
reduces the chance of entering the trachea.
• In horses especially, the tube should be directed properly through the
nasal passage and toward the esophagus.

Purpose
In dogs, passing a stomach tube can be used for:
• diagnosis
• therapy
Required accessories
• mouth opener
• probe/tube
• lubricant
• container/pot
Technique points
• choose the correct tube
• measure from the tip of the nose to the xiphoid cartilage
• use gentle rotation
• never force the tube
• danger: perforation
Important terms
• Xiphoid cartilage = small cartilage at the caudal end of the sternum.
• Perforation = accidental puncture or tearing of the esophagus or
stomach wall.

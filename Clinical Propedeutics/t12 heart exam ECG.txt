-> cough = protective reflex initiated from cough center in the medulla oblongata characterized by explosive expiration of air from the lungs, following brief closure of the glottis, which results from irritation of sensory receptors in the respiratory system
distributed in so-called tusigen zones
- location: pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi (lat. tussis = cough: terminal broncioles and lungs are not tusigen zones)
- phases : deep inspiration followed by closure of the glottis
→ vigorous & forced expiration (glottis still closed)
→ compression of the air in the lungs
→ sudden opening of the glottis: result: explosive expiration
- purpose: to remove foreign material, excess mucus and inflammatory products from the respiratory tract distal from larynx
- usually painless but can also be painful!!!
-> sneezing = protective reflex characterized by sudden, forceful and brief exhallation with purpose to clear nasopharynx induced by irritative stimulation of the nasal mucosa (examples: smoke, gases, foreign bodies, etc.)
- normal: occasional sneezing
- abnormal: persistent sneezing (local disease process: eg. inflammation, tumor)
-> reverse sneezing = protective reflex (mostly) in dogs characterized by spastic inspiratory effort with purpose to clear nasopharynx of irritants
- persistent reverse sneezing is a sign of nasopharyngeal or paranasal sinus disease process
-> snorting = voluntary short explosive expiration through the nasal cavities
- common in cattle & horses
-> stertor (nasalis) = low-pitched snoring sound occurring during breathing and produced by the movements (vibrations) of the soft palate, pharynx or nasopharynx
- physiological in brachycephalic breeds and/or in unconscious state (eg. anesthesia)

Abnormal respiratory sounds
• Harsh breathing (respiratory) sound : consolidation of lung tissue due to congestion, edema
• Bronchial sound over lung : consolidation of lung tissue due to inflamation or atelectasis
• Amphorical breath sound : due to cavernous tissue
• Weakened breath sound or no sound at all (respiratio nulla) : due to emphisema or effusion
• Crackles
◦ Heard more commonly during inspiration (early, late, paninspiratory)
◦ Discontinuous (≤ 250 mscec)
◦ „distant popping” (fine, medium, coarse)
◦ Imply either accumulation of fluid secretions or exudate within airways (pneumonia, bronchitis and severe pulmonary edema)
◦ Old name „rales”
• Wheezes
◦ Most commonly expiratory sounds
◦ Continuous (≥ 250 msec), musical quality
◦ Can be high-pitched or low-pitched
◦ Caused by airflow through a narrow opening
◦ wheezes imply decreased airway lumen diameter due to thickening of airway walls or collapse of airways due to pressure from surrounding pulmonary (diseased) tissue (strictures, compression, foreign bodies
◦ Old name „rhonchi”

ECG

REMINDER CONDUCTION SYSTEM HEART

The cardiac conduction system starts at the sinoatrial node (SA node), the heart’s main pacemaker because it has the fastest
spontaneous depolarization rate. The impulse spreads through the atria via :
-> the Bachmann pathway to the left atrium
-> the internodal pathways to the atrioventricular node (AV node), where it is briefly delayed.

🩺
REMINDER WAVES
Classic ECG waveform showing one cardiac cycle :
•
•
•

•
•
•

It then travels through the bundle of His, which divides into the right and left bundle branches. The left branch further splits into
the anterior and posterior fascicles, before the signal reaches the Purkinje fibers, causing coordinated ventricular contraction.

P wave
atrial depolarization (atria contract) - can be negative but abnormal!
PR interval
time for the impulse to travel from the atria to the ventricles (includes AV node delay)
QRS complex
ventricular depolarization (ventricles contract) - can be reversed (+/-) but abnormal !
- Q = initial small negative deflection
- R = large positive spike
- S = negative deflection after R
ST segment
ventricles are fully depolarized (plateau phase)
T wave
ventricular repolarization (recovery phase) - can be physiologically positive, negative or both (2 phases)
QT interval
total time of ventricular depolarization + repolarization

All these cells can depolarize spontaneously, but since they are slower, the SA node dominates. If it fails, the AV node can take
over as a secondary pacemaker.
+ AV node essential for ventricular depolarization bc annulus fibrosus isolate atria from ventricules and stop propagation of
depolarization btw them

EINTHOVEN’S TRIANGLE

=> Einthoven’s triangle represents the 3 standard limb leads in an ECG :
- Lead I: from right arm (RA, ­) to left arm (LA, +)
- Lead II: from right arm (RA, ­) to left leg (LL, +)
- Lead III: from left arm (LA, ­) to left leg (LL, +)
•

The triangle surrounds the heart, and each lead records the electrical activity from a different angle

•

The green arrow represents the mean electrical axis of ventricular depolarization, which normally points downwards
and to the left (close to Lead II), meaning Lead II usually shows the most positive signal.

=> Key idea: each lead is just a different “view” of the same cardiac electrical activity.

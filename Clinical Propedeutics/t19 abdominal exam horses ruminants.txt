largest stomach compartment and consists of several sacs

the rumen’s environment favors the growth of microbes

the omasum is a globe-shaped structure containing leaves of tissue (like pages in a book)
it absorbs water and other substances from digestive contents

The abomasum
•
•
•

the abomasum is the only compartment that contains glands
these glands release hydrochloric acid and digestive enzymes, needed to breakdown feeds
the abomasum is similar to a nonruminant stomach

Rumination
= complex process of:
- regurgitation
- remastication
- ensalivation
- reswallowing
•
•

cows may spend 35 to 40 percent of each day ruminating (cud chewing)
-> the amount of time spent ruminating depends on the diet : little ruminating occurs when cows eat grain or finely ground rations but when eating long hay, cows may ruminate for several hours
mature cattle spend little time chewing while eating feed. Thus, during rest periods, cows regurgitate (bring up) soft feed wads (cud) to rechew and break into smaller pieces. They also resalivate the feed
and reswallow it. This process makes the feed easier for the microbes to digest

➢ left displacement of the abomasum (LDA)
• the greater curvature of the abomasum passes beneath the rumen and is retained between it and the left abdominal wall, sometimes
reaching beyond the costal arch in the LPF
• the displaced abomasum contains fluid and gas which results with ping
• the ping sounds like a basketball being bounced upon a concrete floor or a steel drum being hit
• the presence of fluid in abomasum can be determined by eliciting fluid-splashing sounds and tinkling sounds (escaping gas bubbles)
on ballottement and auscultation over a wide area
• in lda ruminal movements are reduced in frequency and amplitude and if we hear them they seem to be somehow away

➢ rumen atony and collapse
• diseases of cattle that cause anorexia and rumen stasis may result in the rumen collapse syndrome and a pung over the left abdomen
in the same area as lda (but area is larger and rectangular in shape)
• it could therefore be misdiagnosed as lda
• pung is not associated with abdominal distension
• rumen pack cannot be palpated in lpf
• rectal examination reveals collapsed dorsal sac of the rumen
• rumen movements may be normal or reduced
=> for further differentiation between lda and rumen collapse we can perform abomasocentesis, ultrasonography, endoscopy
-> if sample of fluid has ph 2-4 we can diagnose lda

contractions of the rumen are visible in the left paralumbal fossa
observation of rumen movement at the LPF is only possible in good light and in animals that have a short coat
the moving rumen can be detected indirectly by observing the lateral displacement of the body wall
the alternate rising and sinking of the lpf is associated with the concurrent contraction and relaxation of dorsal and ventral sacs of the rumen

Palpation
•
•
•
•
•

the rumen can be palpated through the abdominal wall at the LPF which is normally slightly concave
we use extended fingers, the palm of the hand or the clenched fist
an impression can be obtained of the size and fullness of the rumen
the mass of rumen contents is commonly known as the rumen pack and have a doughy consistency
above the rumen pack will reveal the normal gas cap

Grain and yesterday’s hay
-> if the gas cap is enlarged the lpf may be convex and the rumen pack not readly palpable
-> it is enlarged in bloat (ruminal tympany) and LPF is distended
-> left-sided displacement of the abomasum (lda) - we can’t feel the rumen pack
-> when rumen contains excessive quantities of fluid the left flank will fluctuate on deep palpation
-> in mature cattle anorexic for several days rumen is smaller and dorsal sac will be collapsed – we can’t feel the rumen pack (sense of emptiness)
Percussion (following the palpation if needed)
•
•

tactile percussion of the abdomen is used – to detect a fluid wave (an undulation of the abdominal wall created by a sharp percussion of the abdominal wall on the opposite side)
wave is detectable when 1/3 or more of abd. cavity contain fluid (e.g. ruptured bladder-uroperitoneum, ascites, diffuse peritonitis)

Ballottement of left abdomen
•
•

the left abdomen can be ballotted with the closed fist over the lower 1/3 of the lpf
auscultation at the same time may reveal fluid-splashing sounds such as LDA or an atonic rumen

Auscultation
• the rumen contraction sounds should be evaluated for intensity, duration and frequency
-> normal : crackling crescendo-decrescendo rolling thunder
-> rumen contractions are reduced in frequency, duration and amplitude or entirely absent:
- hypocalcemia
- endoxemia
- simple indigestion
- acute carbohydrate engorgement
- traumatic reticuloperitonitis
- LDA …
-> presence of fluid-tinkling or splashing sounds along with a static rumen suggests excessive quantity of fluid in rumen (e.g. grain overload, prolonged anorexia)

both of them are resonant sounds indicating the presence of excessive quantities of gas in the lumen of GI
the area being examined is percussed with a flick of the finger, palm or a plexor (a percussion hammer)
the stethoscope is placed immediately adjacent to the area being percussed
the clinican listens for a tympanitic sounds

-> pung - low pitched resonant musical sound (gas filled rumen)

Percussion + simultaneous auscultation
•
•

pings produced by percussion and auscultation represent abnormal accumulations of fluid and gas within abdominal structures
dorsal and ventral of a line from the elbow to the tuber coxae

-> conditions producing pings include:
- abomasal dilatation rda (right-side dilatation and volvulus)
- caecal dilatation or torsion
- gas in the rectum
- pneumoperitoneum
- intestinal tympany in the postparturient cow (postpartum pings)
- intussusception

➢ pressure with the thumb in intercostal spaces (reliability 80%)

=> pings on the right side can be confusing, and location and repeatability of the ping are probably the most useful indicators as to the organ involved
-> while most pings on the left side of the cow are lda, most pings on the right side of the cow are from spiral colon and cecum
-> commonly, cows that are sick, especially with gi disturbances have pings in the right paralumbar fossa
-> a distended or displaced cecum will produce a consistent, large ping that is always palpable per rectum
-> rda and rta (right abomasal torsion) pings are usually cranial to the 8th rib
-> most right-sided abomasal problems have some degree of torsion associated with them, but the overall condition of the cow (heart rate, and degree of dehydration) indicates the severity and degree of torsion of the abomasum

➢ fist pressure test (or knee test) (reliability 85%)
= this test involves applying a sudden upward force with fist supporting with a knee, or with the knee in the area of the xiphisternum

➢ deep percussion (reliability 85%)
= percutaneous percussion intercostal spaces with a heavy percussion hammer from the mid-section of the chest and below to several places on both sides

external palpation and observation of the reticulum is not possible

Metal detectors
•
•

metal detectors have been used to rule out the presence of metal in the structures of the anterior abdomen (wires, nailes..)
however, many normal cattle give positive results due to the presence of harmless metal fragments present in the reticulum

➢ bar test (reliability 85%)
- in the bar test an operator stands either side of the animal
- a padded metal or wooden bar is placed beneath the animal and positioned just behind the xiphisternum
- each operator slowly raises the bar and then lowers it quickly
- the clinician should place a stethoscope over the trachea in the ventral midline of the neck and auscultate for a grunt

➢ withers pinch test (reliability 96%)
- is performed by grasping a fold of skin over the withers area
- this will cause the animal to dip the spine

ORDER OF CLINICAL EXAMS
1. Owner data (name, address, telephone, e-mail): still NOT a part of CE
2. Taking the history (+/- evaluating the environment: farm animals) or anamnesis
3. Patient data: Signalment: data collected for identification purposes: species, breed, age, sex, reproductive status, haircoat colour, congenital or acquired signs: microchip, ear tag, tattoo...
4. Physical
1) distant examination or general inspection
2) particular distant examination of body regions
3) close physical examination examination

GENERAL INSPECTION (IMPRESSION)

=> behaviour and general appearance
How we do it ?
• we are standing back at a distance: looking and listening what our patient is doing
• if the animal is fearful (keep the distance even more!!)
• at first (when we start working) inspection will take some time
• as you will practice → you should get faster and faster...
• crucial is to inspect within „reasonable time”
• we measure body weight (in kg), or estimate it in large animals
• we record all the abnormalities in appropriate form (here: vef. protocol)
• if a patient (mostly cats and dogs) has long hair: for BCS determination we will have to palpate the patient
• small animals (dogs, cats, ferrets etc.): examined on the table, large dogs and large animals: examined on the floor
What we want to evaluate ?
a) level of consciousness (loc): normal, stupor, coma, excited
b) behaviour: normal vs. abnormal (head pressing, circling)
c) posture: normal or abnormal (tetraplegia, scoliosis, head tilt) +/- body shape: normal or abnormal (hydrocephalus)
d) locomotion: normal or abnormal (lack of strength, coordination, presence of pain or mechanical limitation)
e) nutritional condition: normal or abnormal (cachexia, skinny, slim (normal = optimal) overweight to obesity), BCS (body condition score)
• we measure body weight in kg and record it
f) coat: normal or abnormal (dull, dry, hair loss: alopecia, pigmentation)
g) abnormal sounds: normal (nothing) or abnormal (stridor, stertor, voice)
h) abnormalities that stand out: normal (nothing) or abnormal

PARTICULAR DISTANT EXAMINATION OF BODY REGIONS

• Head: sardonic grin (lat. risus sardonicus) and opisthotonos: tetanus
• Neck: enlargement of the guttural pouch (Streptococcus equi) strangles: equine distemper
• Thorax and respirations: polypnea/tachypnea: fear, pain, pleural effusion
• Abdomen (+ mammary glands and external genitalia)
-> distention (abdominal effusion, neoplasm, fetus)
-> gaunt (diarrhea, starvation)
-> ventral edema (infectious equine anemia, congestive heart failure, gangrenous mastitis)
• Limbs and feet: swollen (mammary carcinoma in bitch, hypoproteinemia..)

-> We use:
• Audiovisual inspection
• Palpation (superfitial, deep, ballotment)
• Percussion (direct and indirect, percussion and indirect auscultation)
• Auscultation (direct and indirect)
-> How we do it ?
• As gently as possible
• On standing animal (if possible)
• Order of the examination procedures depends on:
• Species
• History
• Distant examination findings (eg. owner thought that the dog is limping...)
1. Vital signs
-> temperature, pulse, respirations (TPR) - called „TRIAS” (+ in ruminants: ruminal movements)
• Temperature: in °C
• Usually rectal temperature
• We use maximal termometers, old mercury termometers are replaced with digital ones (+ in intensive care units: monitoring devices)
• Normal temperature: Hyper- or hypothermia are symptoms, not a diagnosis
• Pulse
• Respirations
• Ruminal movements
2. Peripheral circulation and state of hydration
-> Inspection of mucous membranes (MM) and skin
- colour: normal or abnormal (pale, cyanotic, hyperemic, icteric..)
- moisture: normal or abnormal (dry or sticky MM)
- CRT: 1-2 sec = normal
• Skin turgor: inspection and palpation of the skin
• Usually palpate the warmth of the skin over the body and extremities (shock: cold limbs; heat stroke: increased temperature)
-> Palpation of:
- arterial pulse
a) Pulse rate: normal or tachy- or bradycardia
b) Pulse rhythm: normal or arrythmia
c) Pulse amplitude (waterhammer)
- auscultation of the heart
a) normal sounds +/- murmurs (some can be normal)
b) abnormal rhythm
- state of peripheral veins
a) jugular veins: normal or engorged (right side heart failure)
3. Lymph nodes

=> The age of the animal also affects the prognosis of the disease
Younger animals generally have a more favorable outcome than older animals
=> Basic principle:
• Younger animals get more often infectious and inflammatory diseases, older get metabolic and neoplastic diseases
• Young cat with diarrhea probably has panleukopenia, old cat can have various diseases including hyperthyroidism
• Young cat with nasal discharge, dyspnea and loud respiratory sounds usually has nasopharyngeal polyp, old has nasal carcinoma

-> Small ruminants and especially pigs are more difficult to examine - for these animals we use an abbreviated examination
-> Sometimes we shorten the clinical examination in: restless or aggressive animals

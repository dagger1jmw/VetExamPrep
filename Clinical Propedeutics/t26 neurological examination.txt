OBSERVATION OF THE PATIENT

- (((deliric, encephalopatic/inappropriate))) = awake but confused
- wake (normal): appropriate interaction
- depressed, apathic (obtunded) = decreased alertness -> every serious disease can cause this
- stupor: reacts only to strong stimuli (pain)
- coma: does not react to any stimuli

The neuro-exam
• mental status + behavior
• gait and postural reactions
• spinal reflexes
• cranial nerves
• palpation and pain perception

Evaluation of postural reactions
= complex reactions that enable our patients to maintain normal posture
• they are not ideal for neurological localization, but they help us find subtle neurological deficits
• tests are performed in the order of increased load on tested limb
1 - proprioceptive positioning (paw position or „knuckling” response)
2 - wheelbarrowing
3 - hopping
4 - hemiwalking
5 - placing response (tactile and visual)
6 - extensor postural thrust
Evaluation of spinal reflexes, muscles size and tone
= test the integrity of the sensory and motor part of reflex arc, and the effect of upper motor neurons (umn) on the reflex
•
•
•

reflexes can be : increased to clonus <> normal <> decreased to absent
we have to determine are there any deficits of upper motor neurons (umn) or lower motor neurons (lmn)
criteria: qualities of the reflexes, muscle tone, intensity and speed of atrophy
Grades :
• + 2 reflex significantly increased, clonus
• + 1 reflex increased
• 0 reflex normal
• 1 reflex decreased
• 2 reflex absent

-> which reflexes to examine?
• thoracic limb
- flexor (withdrawal) reflex : coordinated polysinaptic reflexes: all the flexor muscles of the limb contract
-> test : us pinching gently interdigital skin
-> normal reaction: flexion of shoulder, elbow & carpus - strength & duration of flexor reflex proportional to stimulus
- extensor carpi radialis reflex : most reliable reflex on thoracic limb
-> test: radial nerve integrity & c7-t1 spinal segments
-> normal reaction: slight extension of the carpus
- biceps (brachii) reflex
-> test: musculocutaneous nerve integrity & c6-c8 spinal segments
-> normal reaction: contraction of m. biceps brachii, or slight flexion of the elbow
- triceps reflex : often difficult to elicit in normal animals
-> test: radial nerve integrity & c7-t2 spinal segments
-> normal reaction: visible contraction of m. triceps brachii or slight extension of the elbow
• pelvic limb
- flexsor (withdrawal) reflex : coordinated polysinaptic reflexes: all the flexor muscles of the limb contract
-> test : us pinching gently interdigital skin
-> normal reaction: flexion of the hip, stifle & hock - strength & duration of flexor reflex proportional to stimulus
- patellar reflex : most reliable (except for flexor reflexes)
-> test: femoral nerve integrity & l4-l6 spinal segments
-> normal reaction: single brisk stifle extension
- cranial tibial reflex
-> test: peroneal branch of sciatic nerve & l6-l7 spinal segments
-> normal reaction: flexion of the hock (tarsus)
- gastrocnemius reflex : the least reliable on pelvic limb
-> test: sciatic nerve integrity & l6-s2 spinal segments
-> normal reaction: extension of the hock
• perineal reflex
-> test: sacral nerve (s1-s3) & caudal spinal segments, pudendal nerve branches (perineal & caudal rectal nerve)
-> normal reaction: anal sphincter contraction & tail flexion
• panniculus reflex (m. cutaneus trunci) : cutoff indicates spinal cord lesion 1-(2)-4 segments cranial from cutoff level
-> test: sensory - lateral thoracic nerve & (c8-t1) spinal segments
-> normal reaction: bilateral contraction of. cutaneous trunci muscle: twiching of the skin
Evaluating nociception (= pain perception) - observation, light palpation & superficial and deep pain

• abnormal activity (increased or decreased)

• vocalisation

• Forebrain disease vs. species-specific “normal” behaviour
• Sometimes associated with disorders of other systems — context is essential (e.g. frenzied behaviour in bulls during breeding season, behavioural changes in mares
during dioestrus)
• Subtle signs include: Frequent or continuous yawning (hepatic encephalopathy), Drifting or leaning to one side (asymmetrical encephalopathies), Failure to
recognize familiar animals (e.g. foal ignoring or not recognizing the mare)
• Prominent signs include: Seizures, Circling, Head pressing, Frantic running, Biting of other animals or inanimate objects, Abnormal body posture
• Fluctuation of clinical signs (mild to severe) is common in metabolic encephalopathies (e.g. hyperammonemia)

WHY DOES NEUROLOCALIZATION MATTER ?

(Episodic changes)
• Seizure : an umbrella term encompassing transient, paroxysmal, and episodic phenomena such as: epileptic seizures, syncope, episodes of myotonia, sleep disorders,
pain episodes, vestibular episodes, and paroxysmal dyskinesias.
• These events are frequently missed during clinical examination: a thorough history and video documentation (e.g. smartphone recordings) are of key importance.

2 SIGNALMENT

1. Postural deformities: Often congenital; includes arthrogryposis, flexural limb deformities
2. Opisthotonus: Common in tetanus, May result from rostral cerebellar lesions (e.g. trauma) — without loss of consciousness, Also associated with severe midbrain
damage — typically with stupor or coma
4. Tetanus: Persistent, generalised muscle spasm, Occurs without tremor, Caused by tetanospasmin intoxication
5. Low head/neck carriage: muscular weakness or pain
6. Head tilt (laterocolis): vestibular (or cerebellar dysfunction)
7. Pleurothotonus: head, neck and body deviation to one side (may be seen with forebrain or brainstem disease)
8. Extended head/neck posture (“star-gazing behaviour”): severe forebrain disease (eg. polioencephalomalacia or basilar empyema)

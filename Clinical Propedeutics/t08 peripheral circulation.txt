PERIPHERAL CIRCULATION ASSESSMENT

• peripheral perfusion can be examined by measuring capillary refill time (crt)
• crt is defined as the time required for a distal capillary bed (oral mucosa) regaining its color after pressure has been applied to cause blanching
• nonpigmented mucosa of the upper lip or gingival mucosa
• normal crt is 1 to 1.5 (up to 2) seconds
• slow return of color may indicate poor perfusion

• exercise intolerance is ultimately a neuromuscular phenomenon resulting from extreme metabolic stress at the level of muscle fibers
➢ disorders that affect oxygen transport (cardiopulmonary system)
➢ disorders that affect mechanical efficiency (locomotor system - lameness)
=> two types of tests
-> treadmill test
-> field test
• dyspnea, fatigue, and a prolonged elevation in heart rate following exercise are signs suggestive of cardiorespiratory insufficiency
• additionally, ecg, echocardiography and endoscopy can be performed
• measurement of lactate levels

The bright red scenario

• the 6 minute walk test (6mwt) is used in people as a functional assessment of cardiopulmonary reserves
• it is not designed to evaluate maximal effort, but instead to more closely evaluate the activities of daily living
• the test is performed by measuring the distance an individual may walk comfortably over six minutes
• importantly, the 6mwt is not performed in those individuals that are short of breath, but rather in those that are potentially affected
with a cardiopulmonary disease that might affect exercise tolerance

SYSTEMIC ARTERIAL BLOOD PRESSURE
• arterial blood pressure = pressure exerted by blood against the arterial wall
• systolic arterial blood pressure = peak pressure during the heart contraction
• diastolic arterial blood pressure = minimal pressure between the heart contractions
• mean arterial blood pressure = average blood pressure in a individual animal during one heart cycle; perfusion pressure

Jugular pulsation
• during each heart cycle, three venous pressure waves can be recorded:
- a wave
- c wave
- v wave
• the a wave is the result of atrial contraction; the c wave is synchronous with carotid pulse; the v wave marks the end of the ventricular systole
• the associated movements are called venous pulse

a wave – atrial contraction
c wave – tricuspid valve closure;
x descent – atrial relaxation
v wave – ventricular ejection
=> poor man’s blood pressure monitor = pulse quality
• used for exclusion of arterial hypotension
• palpation of dorsal pedal artery pulse generally correlates with sap >90 mmhg
• palpation of femoral artery pulse in the absence of dorsal pedal artery pulse – sap >60<90 mmhg
• inability to palpate femoral artery pulse – sap <60 mmhg

=> although commonly referred to as venous pulsation it is actually a variation in the degree of jugular distension
-> negative venous stasis : when the jugular vein becomes distended above the compression point, with the section of the vein below emptying with the heart beat
-> positive venous stasis : in advanced cases of venous stasis the segment of the jugular vein below the compression point does not empty, but remains just as full
Abdominojugular (hepatojugular) reflux (reflex)
• apply firm pressure to the cranial abdomen while the animal is standing quietly – this transiently increases venous return
• jugular distension that persists while abdominal pressure is applied – positive (abnormal test)

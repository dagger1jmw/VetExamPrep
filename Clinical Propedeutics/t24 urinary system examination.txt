ANURIA : complete cessation of urine formation by the kidneys
OLIGURIA : reduced output of urine
POLYURIA : formation and elimination of abnormally large quantities of urine (many non-urinary diseases may be associated with polyuria)
POLLAKIURIA : abnormally frequent passage of urine; it may occur with or without an increase in the volume of urine excreted
DYSURIA : painful or difficult urination
STRANGURIA : slow and painful urination
(MACRO)HEMATURIA : the presence of blood in urine
PIGMENTURIA : presence of pigments in urine - bilirubin (bilirubinuria), myoglobin (myoglobinuria), hemoglobin (hemoglobinuria) etc.

MEDICAL IMAGING OF URINARY TRACT

➢ urinary system consists of the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra
➢ main function à production and excretion of urine
➢ the kidneys represent the upper urinary tract
➢ the ureters, bladder and urethra represent the lower urinary tract

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION OF THE URINARY TRACT IN DOGS AND CATS
- signalment and history should precede the physical exam
- history should include information on: the frequency of urination, volume of urine produced, changes in water intake, and the appearance (color and odor) of the urine
- owners more easily notice abnormally increased water intake (polydipsia), than abnormally increased urine production (polyuria)
- polydipsia usually occurs if there is significant polyuria
- do not forget to ask about possible exposure to nephrotoxins and drugs
WATER CONSUMPTION IN DOGS AND CATS
• normal consumption in a controlled environment is 6-25 ml/kg/day
• polydipsia = >100 ml/kg/day

URINE PRODUCTION IN DOGS AND CATS
• normal urine production is 26-44 ml/kg/day
• polyuria = >50 ml/kg/day

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION OF THE URINARY TRACT IN LARGE ANIMALS
1. taking a history,
2. performing a distant examination of the external genitalia,
3. visual observation of the act of urination,
4. physical examination, primarily by inspection and palpation, and,
5. urinalysis.

CLINICAL TIP (Cow)
Only left kidney and bladder (if full) should be palpable rectally and only both kidneys and bladder (esp. surroundings) should be visible with ultrasounds

=> major clinical and historical findings include dysuria, abnormal urine (color and consistency), and changes in the volume of urine produced and voided
Distant examination - Behavioural aspects

- although we are talking about examination of the urinary tract, a complete physical examination, including a rectal examination and an ocular fundic examination should be done
- diagnostic aids, such as medical imaging and laboratory investigations complement the physical examination
- physical evaluation of the urinary tract in dogs and cats is carried out by visual inspection, palpation (of the abdomen) and rectal examination
- do not forget to collect a urine sample for analysis!

Palpation of the kidneys
- evaluation of the kidneys for location, size, shape, consistency and pain
- in dogs kidneys are bean-shaped, and in cats are more spherical
- enlargement of one or both kidneys due to infectious, neoplastic, or anomalous conditions may facilitate their palpation
- in cats, several diseases are associated with enlargement of the kidneys – renomegaly: polycystic kidney disease, renal lymphoma and granulomatous nephritis (associated with feline infectious peritonitis)
- most chronic diseases, causing mainly chronic renal failure, are associated with normal or small kidneys
• both kidneys are readily palpable in most cats and are
located in the dorsal portion of the cranial quadrant of the
abdomen
• right kidney is located ventral to the L1-L4 and left lies
ventral to the L2-L5

• in the dog sometimes only the left kidney
is readily palpable, by deep palpation of
the craniodorsal abdomen
• right kidney is in level with L1-L3, and
the left, being freely movable, is caudal, in
level with L2-L4

Examination of the kidneys
- because of the considerable thickness and rigidity of the abdominal wall kidneys cannot be palpated per abdomen in cattle and horses, and compact muscling of swine also precludes external palpation
- in cattle and horses kidneys are examined by rectal palpation
- in sheep and goats kidney are evaluated by external palpation, since rectal examination is not possible because of the small anus

-> in the cattle kidneys are normally lobulated
-> only the caudal half of the normal left kidney can be
palpated on rectal examination, ventral to the L3-L5

-> in the horse the left kidney is palpable by rectal
examination ventral to the last rib, and L1-L2 (L3)
-> the right kidney is somewhat firmly fixed in position,
and is often not palpable

-> the kidneys of swine are smooth an bean-shaped, encased in fat
-> in swine palpation of the kidneys, external or internal (rectal) is not possible
Palpation of the urinary bladder
- can be palpated in most dogs and cats, unless it is empty or the animal is markedly obese
- it is evaluated for position in the abdomen, degree of distension, evidence of pain, thickness of the wall, and sometimes for the presence of intramural/intraluminal masses
• one-handed technique is best
• very small/very large bladders are usually difficult to feel
• a medium-sized bladder is normally palpable in the caudal abdominal quadrant
• smooth, pear-shaped structure
Urethra
- urethra of female dogs and cats is difficult to examine
- digital vaginal examination can be done in order to evaluate the urethral orifice, best with the aid of a speculum and light source
- in male cats examination of the urethra is limited to inspection of the distal portion of the penile urethra
- the urethra of male dogs can be examined by inspection and palpation; the urethral meatus may be examined by exteriorizing the penis, and the perineal portion may be palpated under the skin
- the pelvic urethra is examined by rectal examination
- evaluation of position, degree of distension, evidence of pain, thickness of wall, and presence of masses
- normal urethra is a smooth, tubular structure, on the floor of the pelvis

-> sheep and goats the kidneys are bean-shaped, without lobulations
-> kidneys can be palpated externally by placing a hand in the upper margin of each paralumbar fossa immediately behind the last rib, followed by moving of each hand in a caudomedial direction across the fossa
Examination of the ureters
- the ureters in normal cattle and horses are virtually impossible to palpate because of their small diameter and subperitoneal course
- if the ureters are palpable the likelihood of an abnormality is high – bilateral enlargement is caused by inflammatory process (e.g. pyelonephritis or urethritis); unilateral enlargement is mostly caused by an ureteral urolith or abscess
Examination of the urinary bladder
- in normal cattle/horses the bladder is pear-shaped, narrowest at its attachment with the pelvic urethra which lies on the floor of the pelvis
- it is usually not palpable because it remains relatively empty -> has a slightly corrugated serosal surface
- in the cow/mare the bladder is even more difficult to locate because of the overlying reproductive tract
- in small ruminants and swine the bladder has the same features as that of cattle and horses
- a readily palpable bladder can be an abnormal finding, or can reflect a simple retention
Examination of the urethra
- in large animals the penis and associated urethra can only be inspected along the length of glans penis to the fornix of the prepuce; palpation is possible along most of its length
- in bulls and steers the preputial hairs should be examined for signs of dryness (no recent urination) and presence of crystals on the hairs (crystalluria and urolithiasis); the penis should be palpated (swelling, pain) from the prepuce to
just above the scrotum
- in sheep and goats penis can be exteriorized by pushing the sigmoid flexure cranially with one hand while simultaneously retracting the prepuce with the other hand; in castrated bucks and rams penis is difficultto exteriorize
- in horses the penis may be exteriorized by exerting slow steady retraction with a hand grasping the glans
- the penis in conscious boar and barrow cannot easily be exteriorized, but under sedation it may be exposed with the aid of retractors directed through the preputial opening; it has no glans and is spirally twisted
- in the female the urethra is located on the midline of the floor of the vagina within some 10 cm inside the vulvar lips
- the suburethral diverticulum is present in the cow, ewe, dow, and sow
- urethral diameter is usually sufficient to allow insertion of a finger
- palpable abnormalities of the female urethra are usually uncommon

Appeareance and physical characteristics
-> color :
CLINICAL TIP
• Normal urine is pale yellow to light amber, and is generally clear to slightly cloudy
Considered transparent if possible to read through it
• Urine that is dark yellow usually suggests dehydration
• Urine that is very pale yellow or clear suggests the animal has PUPD
• Urine that is any color other than yellow (for example orange, red, or brown-black) may contain substances not normally found in healthy urine and may reflect injury or underlying disease
-> turbidity (cloudiness) :
• Increased turbidity or cloudiness indicates that there are cells or other solid material in the urine.
• Normal horse urine is cloudy. This is due to the calcium carbonate crystals present in urine
• Normal horse urine is foamy. This is due to the presence of totally normal mucus in urine. The mucus helps to prevent the calcium carbonate crystals from forming stones.
-> Urine-specific gravity (USG) = an index of concentration — the higher the USG, the more concentrated the urine — and is measured using a refractometer
• The USG of dogs can vary widely throughout the day, but the highest USG (usually >1.035- 1.040) typically is found in the morning, before the dog eats and drinks.
• USG varies less throughout the day in cats, and cats typically have moderately concentrated urine when eating dry food (usually ≥1.035)
• USG of 1.040 or higher is expected animals that are dehydrated
• If the USG at presentation is in the hyposthenuric (<1.007) or isosthenuric (1.007-1.014) range further investigation is needed

-> Bilirubin:
- Bilirubin is a substance that is produced in the liver and normally excreted in the bile
- Bilirubin is not found in the urine of healthy cats but may be found in small quantities in the urine of healthy dogs. Abnormal amounts of bilirubin in the urine are associated with liver disease or red blood cell destruction (“hemolysis”).
Examination of sediment
• The most common things found in urine sediment are :
-> red blood cells
• It is within normal limits to see low numbers of erythrocytes (red blood cells, RBCs) in urine, or more in samples obtained by cystocentesis (iatrogenic contamination) or in females in estrus
• RBCs in increased numbers in the urine (hematuria) are indicative of a haemorrhage
-> white blood cells
• It is within normal limits to see low numbers (5/hpf) of leukocytes (white blood cells, WBCs) in urine. WBCs in increased numbers in the urine (pyuria) indicate inflam- mation
-> crystals
• Urine crystals may be seen as an incidental finding (e.g., struvite) or pathologic finding (e.g., calcium oxalate monohydrate in ethylene glycol toxicity, ammonium biurate in patients with portosystemic shunts, etc.).
• Crystalluria does not correlate highly with urolithiasis
-> bacteria and other organisms
• It is within normal limits to see low numbers of bacteria in voided samples (especially cocci) or samples obtained from the floor, table, or litter box (mixed bacteria)
• It is not normal to see organisms in samples obtained by cystocentesis
• Pyuria is expected in cases of UTI but not always found, especially in immunocompromised patients
• Not seeing any bacteria does not exclude the possibility of a urinary tract infection (UTI)
• A culture is a more sensitive means of detection
• Fungi or parasite eggs may rarely be found in urine samples as pathogens or contaminants
-> tissue cells from different parts of the urinary system
• It is within normal limits to see low numbers of epithelial cells in urine samples
• Normal epithelial cells include transitional cells (urothelium) in samples obtained by cystocentesis, transitional cells and/or squamous epithelial cells in voided samples or samples obtained by catheter, and even rare renal tubular cells in any type of sample
• Large, atypical epithelial cells, or high numbers of any type of epithelial cells, are an abnormal finding, and may indicate further testing to evaluate for possible urinary tract neoplasia or other underlying disease
-> casts = cylinder-shaped forms that literally are casts of the renal tubules (the tubular lumen acts as a mold) - The presence of casts in the urine is sometimes called cylinduria.
• Casts are composed mainly of a mucoprotein secreted by cells lining certain parts of the nephron, and often have cells incorporated within them
• Casts are classified as:
- cellular (individual cells are discernible),
- granular (cells have partially dis- integrated),
- waxy (cells have disintegrated and the casts have a smooth, waxy texture),
- hyaline (casts are acel- lular, clear, and colorless).
• The presence of rare hyaline or granular casts is considered within normal limits; otherwise, cylinduria indicates urinary tract (usually tubular) pathology hyaline (casts are acel- lular, clear, and colorless).
-> Small amounts of mucus and miscellaneous debris are often found in free-catch samples
-> Rarely, parasite eggs are found in urine
